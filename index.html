Credit, in plain language

No mystery, no lecture. What a score is, how to see yours free, and how to build one from zero.

What a credit score actually is

It's a number from 300 to 850 that summarizes one thing: do you pay what you owe, on time. Landlords, phone companies, and lenders check it.

It is not a judgment of you. It's a payment history, and histories can be rebuilt, starting any time.

See your report free, the official way

Every week, free, from the only official site: annualcreditreport.com. Lookalike sites charge money or sell you things. Skip them.

The report shows what's being said about you. The score costs extra there; you usually don't need it; fixing the report fixes the score.

Build from zero with a secured card

A secured card is a credit card with training wheels: you put down a deposit (often $200), and that's your limit. The bank risks nothing, so approval is easy even with no history.

Use it for one small thing, like a phone bill, and pay it in full every month. In about six months, there's a payment history where there was none.

Fix mistakes: free, and they're common

Reports have errors more often than you'd think: debts that aren't yours, things paid long ago. Disputing is free, online or by mail, and the bureau must answer within 30 days.

Never pay a "credit repair" company. Everything they charge for, you can do free.

What helps, what hurts

Helps most: paying on time, every time, even the minimum. Keeping old accounts open. Using only a little of your limit.

Hurts most: missed payments, collections, maxed-out cards. One late payment fades; a pattern doesn't.

Free help that's actually free

Four doors, all free, all real. Start with the first one if you're not sure.

Any kind of help, one call: dial 211 for free, 24/7. They point you to food, housing, and bill help near you. Online: findhelp.org
